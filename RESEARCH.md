# Research — every version of the strategy, every test

This is the full walkthrough. [README.md](README.md) has the summary table;
this file has the mechanics — exact rules, exact formulas, exact numbers, and
why each idea was kept, tuned, or thrown out. Every number below is read
straight from `results/phaseN/PHASEN_REPORT.md`, which is regenerated by
running `src/phaseN_*.py` — nothing here is hand-typed from memory.

**Conventions that hold across every version tested:** weekly candles
(`W-FRI` resample of daily OHLC), long only, no shorting, no leverage unless
stated, no transaction costs unless stated (Phase 12 adds them as a stress
test). Execution convention: a signal computed from a weekly **close** is
acted on at the **next week's Monday open** — a 2-week shift applied to
open-to-open returns (`state_backtest` in `src/backtest.py`). This models
"you see Friday's close over the weekend and place the order Monday morning."

**Universe (16 sleeves):** SPY, QQQ, DIA, IWM, `^NDX` (NAS100), `^GSPC`
(SP500), `GC=F` (gold), `SI=F` (silver), `CL=F` (oil), `EURUSD=X`, `GBPUSD=X`,
`JPY=X` (USDJPY), and four sector ETFs — XLK (tech), XLU (utilities), XLI
(industrials), XLB (materials) — standing in for "large-cap tech / utilities /
industrials / materials" from the original brief. Sector ETFs were chosen over
picking individual stocks specifically to avoid survivorship bias (a stock
list assembled today already knows which companies survived).

## Phase 1 — the book's system, exactly as written

**Rule:** compute a 22-week SMA and a 55-week SMA of the weekly close. When
fast crosses above slow, go long at next Monday's open. When fast crosses
below slow, exit at next Monday's open. That's the entire system — no stops,
no sizing, no filters. Implemented in `src/backtest.py::cross_backtest`.

**Result, longest-history assets:**

| Asset | Period | CAGR | Sharpe | MaxDD | Trades |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| SP500 | 1929–2026 | 6.0% | 0.50 | −47.2% | 36 |
| NAS100 | 1986–2026 | 12.3% | 0.69 | −41.2% | 16 |
| SPY | 1994–2026 | 11.1% | 0.83 | −31.8% | 10 |
| GOLD | 2001–2026 | 9.4% | 0.67 | −31.1% | 10 |
| EURUSD | 2004–2026 | −1.1% | −0.15 | −30.7% | 12 |
| GBPUSD | 2004–2026 | −1.1% | −0.15 | −29.7% | 14 |

Full 16-asset table with Sortino, Calmar, profit factor, win rate, exposure,
and negative-year counts: `results/phase1/PHASE1_REPORT.md`.

**Trade structure — where the profit actually comes from.** For every asset
the top 10% of trades produce a wildly disproportionate share of net profit:
SPY 43%, NAS100 62%, USDJPY 76%, and on Oil a single trade accounts for more
than the entire net result (111% — the other 16 trades net negative). SPY's
average winner is +56% against an average loser of −2.4%; NAS100's largest
winner was +596% (the entire dot-com-to-2000 uptrend held in one trade). This
is the mechanical signature of trend following: a small number of long,
correctly-held winners pay for a large number of short, wrong whipsaws.

**Long-only FX is broken.** EURUSD and GBPUSD are net unprofitable over 20+
years (CAGR −1.1% each, Sharpe −0.15). Currency trends are two-sided —
cutting the short side removes roughly half the edge on an asset class with
no long-run drift the way equities/gold have. Equities and gold "work" partly
because the filter free-rides on their positive secular drift; this doesn't
hold for FX. **This is a structural finding that shapes every later phase**
(the portfolios in Phase 6 still keep FX for diversification, but the FTMO
config never expects FX to carry its own weight).

**Negative years / underwater time.** Roughly 30% of years are negative on
every asset (SP500: 32 of 98). Underwater periods run long: GBPUSD 21.4
years, Oil 18.1 years, NAS100 12.0 years. A raw long-only SMA-cross book
system is not tradeable under any capital-preservation constraint as-is —
this motivates everything from Phase 5 onward.

## Phase 3 — is 22/55 special? (195 combos tested)

**Grid:** fast ∈ {10, 15, 20, 22, 30, 40, 50} weeks × slow ∈ {40, 50, 55, 70,
100, 150} weeks (fast < slow only) × 5 MA types = 195 valid combinations.
**MA types tested**, all in `src/backtest.py`:

- **SMA** — plain rolling mean.
- **EMA** — `pandas.ewm(span=n)`, exponential decay.
- **WMA** — linearly weighted rolling mean (`src/backtest.py::wma`), most
  recent bar weighted `n`, oldest weighted `1`.
- **HMA** (Hull) — `WMA(2·WMA(close, n/2) − WMA(close, n), sqrt(n))`, designed
  to reduce lag relative to SMA/EMA.
- **KAMA** (Kaufman adaptive) — `src/backtest.py::kama`; smoothing constant
  is a function of the efficiency ratio `|Δprice_n| / Σ|Δprice|`, so it moves
  fast in a trend and flattens in chop.

**Scoring:** median Sharpe across all 16 assets (not mean — one asset having
an extreme run shouldn't crown a parameter set), common 155-week warmup so
every combo is judged on identical weeks.

**Finding — a flat plateau, not a peak.** The book's 22/55 SMA scores median
Sharpe 0.54 and sits at the **90th percentile** of all 195 combos tested. But
93% of the SMA/EMA/WMA grid scores Sharpe > 0.3, and the whole plateau sits
in a narrow 0.48–0.58 band (see `results/phase3/charts/heatmaps.png`). SMA,
EMA and WMA are functionally interchangeable (grid median 0.51–0.52 each).
**HMA is worse across the board** (grid median 0.38, best cell 0.49) — its
reduced-lag design catches turns earlier but also catches more false ones.
KAMA sits between (median 0.47).

**Verdict:** don't optimize this. The grid being flat is the good outcome — a
sharp isolated peak would be a red flag for curve-fitting. 22/55 SMA is kept
as-is; the top-10 table in the report is shown explicitly labeled "do NOT
cherry-pick" because nearby cells (WMA 50/70 at 0.58, SMA 40/50 at 0.56) are
statistically indistinguishable from the baseline given how flat the surface
is.

## Phase 4 — twelve rival trend signals

Each signal replaces the 22/55 cross as the long/flat gate; execution
convention unchanged. Formulas (`src/phase4_trend_signals.py`):

- **Donchian breakouts** (20w / 40w / 50w / 100w): long while price ≥ rolling
  N-week high; exit when price ≤ rolling (N/2)-week low.
- **Time-series momentum**: `close.shift(skip) / close.shift(n) − 1 > 0` for
  n = 26w (6m), 39w (9m), 52w (12m), and 12-1 (52w lookback skipping the most
  recent 4 weeks, the classic momentum-crash-avoidance variant).
- **Regression slope**: rolling 52-week OLS of `log(close)` on time; long
  when slope > 0, optionally gated by R² > 0.5.
- **Efficiency ratio** (`ER26>0.3 & up`): `|close_t − close_{t-26}| / Σ|Δclose|`
  over 26 weeks, thresholded at 0.3, ANDed with price above its 26-week-ago
  level.
- **Hurst exponent**: rolling variance-ratio estimate (`H = ½log₂(var(4-week
  sums)/var(1-week)) / 2`) thresholded at 0.5, ANDed with an uptrend filter.

**Result (median Sharpe across 16 assets):**

| Signal | Median Sharpe | Worst asset | % assets positive |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slope > 0 (52w regression) | 0.52 | −0.15 | 88% |
| 12-1 momentum | 0.52 | −0.15 | 88% |
| MA 22/55 cross (baseline) | 0.52 | −0.08 | 88% |
| 6-month momentum | 0.51 | +0.05 | 100% |
| Donchian 50w | 0.47 | −0.30 | 88% |
| Hurst > 0.5 & up | 0.35 | −0.25 | 88% |
| Donchian 20w | 0.32 | +0.06 | 88% |
| Slope>0 & R²>0.5 | 0.31 | −0.21 | 88% |
| Efficiency-ratio gate | 0.18 | −0.49 | 94% |

**No family dominates.** MA cross, 12-1 momentum, and raw regression slope
are statistically tied at ~0.52. Donchian breakouts are consistently a notch
worse — they enter later (need a fresh high, not just a cross) and this costs
more than it saves. **Trend-*strength* filters actively hurt when used as the
sole signal**: gating on R² or efficiency ratio throws away too many
legitimate but noisy trends (GBPUSD under the ER gate drops to −0.49 Sharpe).
This result directly predicts the Phase 7 regime-filter rejection.

## Phase 5 — eighteen sizing schemes

**Method:** hold the 22/55 SMA signal fixed; only the position size changes.
Weight = `min(target_vol / realized_vol, cap)`, applied while the signal is
long, zero otherwise (`src/phase5_vol_sizing.py::sized_returns`).

- **fixed 1x** — the book's implicit sizing (always full position).
- **atr** — `ATR(14) / close`, annualized by `√52`, as the vol estimate.
- **realized26** — rolling 26-week std of returns, annualized.
- **ewma** — 26-week-span EWMA std of returns, annualized (reacts faster
  than the flat rolling window).
- Each vol estimator × targets **{10%, 15%, 20%}** annual × caps **{1x, 2x}**
  = 18 sizing configs, plus fixed 1x = 19 total.

**Result — portfolio level (equal-weight of all 16 sleeves), common window:**

| Sizing | Sharpe | CAGR | MaxDD | Calmar |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ewma t=15% cap=1x | 0.77 | 6% | −16% | 0.36 |
| fixed 1x (no sizing) | 0.77 | 8% | **−24%** | 0.32 |
| ewma t=10% cap=1x | 0.76 | 4% | **−12%** | 0.37 |
| atr t=10% cap=1x | 0.75 | 3% | −10% | 0.30 |

**Sharpe is essentially unchanged by vol sizing** (0.74–0.77 across all 19
configs) — the risk-adjusted return of any one asset's trend edge doesn't
improve. **What changes is drawdown**: going from unsized (−24% maxDD) to
EWMA 10%-target sizing (−12% maxDD) **halves the worst-case loss for the same
Sharpe**, because sizing shrinks exposure precisely during the high-volatility
periods that produce the worst drawdowns. ATR, realized-26w, and EWMA
estimators are near-interchangeable; EWMA was adopted for the production
config because it reacts fastest to a fresh volatility regime, which matters
more once the FTMO drawdown floor (Phase 11) is added on top.

**Adopted:** EWMA-26 vol estimator, 10% annual target, cap 1x (no leverage
at the sleeve level).

## Phase 6 — five portfolios, correlation, risk contribution

Sleeves = Phase 5's adopted sizing (EWMA-26, 10% target, cap 1x) applied per
asset; portfolios are equal-weight combinations of sleeve returns.

| Portfolio | Assets | Sharpe | CAGR | MaxDD | Ann. vol | Avg pairwise corr |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| A: QQQ only | 1 | 0.79 | 8% | −15% | 10% | 1.00 |
| B: Stock sectors | TECH/UTIL/INDU/MATL | 0.71 | 6% | −17% | 8% | 0.53 |
| C: Indices | 6 equity indices | 0.70 | 6% | −18% | 9% | 0.84 |
| D: Global macro | SPY+gold+silver+oil+3FX | 0.65 | 3% | **−8%** | 4% | **0.16** |
| E: Everything | all 16 | 0.76 | 4% | −12% | 6% | 0.36 |

**Concentrated bets look better on Sharpe alone** (A: QQQ-only hits 0.79)
because QQQ happened to have a strong multi-decade run — but that's
survivorship risk in miniature, one instrument's idiosyncratic history. **What
diversification actually buys is shown by D and E**: Portfolio D (global
macro, avg corr 0.16) achieves the lowest volatility (4%) and shallowest
drawdown (−8%) of any portfolio, at the cost of lower CAGR. Portfolio E (all
16, avg corr 0.36) is the practical sweet spot — Sharpe close to the
concentrated portfolios (0.76) but at 6% volatility and −12% max drawdown
instead of −15% to −24% for anything narrower. Risk contribution (equal-weight
Portfolio E) is dominated by the equity sleeves (SPY 9.8%, TECH 9.4%, DIA
9.3% each) despite equal capital weights, because equities carry higher
realized vol than FX (USDJPY contributes only 1.1% of risk) — a reminder that
equal-weight ≠ equal-risk. See `results/phase6/PHASE6_REPORT.md` for the full
16×16 correlation matrix. **This is the single biggest lever pulled in the
whole program**: nothing else in Phases 3, 4, 7, 8, or 9 moves Sharpe or
drawdown by nearly as much as simply holding 16 uncorrelated sleeves instead
of one.

## Phase 7 — eight regime filters, all ANDed onto the baseline signal

Formulas in `src/phase7_regime_filters.py`:

- **ADX(14) > 20 / > 25** — classic Wilder trend-strength gate.
- **MA distance > 1% / > 3%** — `fast/slow − 1` must clear a minimum gap.
- **Trend-strength percentile > 30%** — rolling 104-week percentile rank of
  the MA distance.
- **Vol expanding / vol calm** — `ATR(14)/ATR(52)` above or below 1.

| Filter | Median Sharpe | Median MaxDD | Total trades | Whipsaws (<8w) | Avg exposure |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| MA dist > 1% | 0.53 | −34% | 220 | 15 | 62% |
| **baseline (no filter)** | **0.52** | **−36%** | **198** | **11** | **69%** |
| ADX > 20 | 0.52 | −33% | 560 | 131 | 54% |
| strength percentile > 30% | 0.49 | −33% | 258 | 19 | 57% |
| vol calm | 0.44 | −28% | 625 | 285 | 35% |
| vol expanding | 0.36 | −31% | 584 | 267 | 34% |

**Rejected wholesale.** The one filter that ties the baseline (MA dist > 1%,
+0.01 Sharpe) is noise at this sample size. Every ADX/vol-based filter
roughly **triples to quintuples the trade count** (198 → 560–625) by flipping
in and out around its threshold, and whipsaws (round trips under 8 weeks)
explode 12–25x with no compensating Sharpe gain — several filters are
outright worse (vol expanding: 0.36 vs 0.52). The explanation connects
directly to Phase 4: the slow 22/55 cross *already is* a regime filter (it's
slow specifically so it doesn't react to chop), so stacking a second, faster
regime gate on top mostly adds noise around the first one's own threshold
crossings.

## Phase 8 — five entry variants after the signal fires

Entry and exit are otherwise identical to the baseline; only *when* the long
position actually starts changes (`src/phase8_entries.py`):

- **immediate** — next Monday open (the book's rule, baseline).
- **confirm2** — wait two additional weekly closes above the signal before
  entering (skips the trade entirely if the cross reverses within 2 weeks).
- **pullback3 / pullback5** — wait for a 3%/5% dip below the signal week's
  close, up to an 8-week timeout, then enter at market if the pullback never
  comes (without the timeout, pullback entries would silently skip the
  strongest, non-retracing trends — the ones that matter most).
- **donchian4** — wait for a fresh 4-week price high after the signal before
  entering.

| Entry | Median Sharpe | Median CAGR | Median MaxDD | Median Calmar |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| confirm2 | 0.53 | 7% | −35% | 0.19 |
| donchian4 | 0.52 | 7% | −35% | 0.17 |
| pullback3 | 0.52 | 7% | −36% | 0.18 |
| immediate (book) | 0.52 | 7% | −36% | 0.15 |
| pullback5 | 0.51 | 7% | −34% | 0.18 |

**No meaningful edge anywhere** — all five variants land within a 0.02 Sharpe
band. On weekly bars, a few days of entry timing is noise relative to
multi-year trend duration. Confirmation and pullback entries occasionally
improve Calmar slightly by avoiding the worst immediate whipsaws, but not
enough to justify added complexity (missed entries, timeout logic). **Kept:
immediate Monday open**, exactly as the book specifies.

## Phase 9 — eight exit variants, entry held fixed

All exits also honor the underlying cross-down (a stop never *overrides* the
regime signal, it only tightens it); re-entry is allowed on a fresh 4-week
high while the regime stays long, so a stop-out doesn't permanently end a
still-valid trend episode (`src/phase9_exits.py`):

- **MA cross (book)** — baseline, exit only when 22 crosses below 55.
- **ATR trail 3x / 4x / 5x** — chandelier exit: `close < highest_close_since_entry − k·ATR(14)`.
- **Donchian low 10w / 20w** — exit below the rolling N-week low.
- **Time stop 52w** — force exit one year after entry regardless of price.
- **Vol stop 1.5x** — exit if `ATR(14)/ATR(52) > 1.5` (volatility spike).

Ranked by **Calmar** (return/drawdown), never by Sharpe or return alone:

| Exit | Median Sharpe | Median CAGR | Median MaxDD | Median Calmar |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ATR trail 4x | 0.53 | 7% | −33% | **0.19** |
| Time stop 52w | 0.53 | 7% | −36% | 0.17 |
| Vol stop 1.5x | 0.52 | 6% | −33% | 0.17 |
| MA cross (book) | 0.52 | 7% | −36% | 0.15 |
| Donchian 10w low | 0.39 | 5% | −32% | 0.14 |

**A wide trail helps a little; a tight one hurts a lot.** 4x ATR mildly beats
the book's pure cross-exit on Calmar (0.19 vs 0.15) by cutting a few of the
worst drawdowns. But the 10-week Donchian low — a *tight* stop — is the worst
performer of the whole set (Calmar 0.14, Sharpe drops to 0.39): it exits
profitable trends on normal pullbacks and gets whipsawed back in. Phase 10
explains exactly why tight stops backfire.

## Phase 10 — MAE / MFE: how much pain precedes the gain

For every one of the 206 closed baseline trades across all 16 assets, MAE
(maximum adverse excursion — the worst close reached vs. entry price while in
the trade) and MFE (best) were measured (`src/phase10_drawdown_survival.py`).

- Winners' MAE: **median −2.3%**, 75th percentile −5.1%, worst single case
  −18.6% (a trade that went nearly 19% against entry before eventually
  closing positive).
- **27% of eventual winners first went more than 5% underwater**; 9% went
  more than 10% underwater before recovering.
- Of trades whose MAE was worse than −10%, **24% still finished positive.**
- Losers' MFE median +2.4% — most losing trades were briefly green first,
  meaning the *exit*, not the entry, is what turned them into losses.

**This is the direct, data-backed reason Phase 9's tight stops fail**: a stop
set anywhere near typical winner MAE (5–10%) would have closed out roughly a
quarter to a third of the trades that eventually became winners. Consecutive
losing-trade streaks reach up to 6 (GBPUSD) and 4 (Gold); most equity sleeves
never exceed 1–2 in a row. The portfolio-level implication, carried straight
into Phase 11: **risk has to be managed by sizing and diversification across
sleeves, not by tight per-trade stops** — a stop tight enough to matter also
guts the edge.

## Phase 11 — the FTMO overlay (five configurations)

Base = Portfolio E (16 EWMA-sized sleeves, equal weight). Three independent
overlay mechanisms, implemented in `src/phase11_ftmo.py::overlay`, applied to
the portfolio return stream week by week:

- **Scale** — a constant multiplier on every sleeve's exposure (shrinks
  overall volatility).
- **Drawdown-linear cut** — `exposure *= max(0, 1 + dd/8%)`; exposure falls
  linearly as live drawdown deepens and hits exactly **zero at −8%**,
  guaranteeing the strategy de-risks itself before an −8% drawdown can become
  a −10% one.
- **Vol brake** — if trailing 8-week realized portfolio vol exceeds 1.5× the
  target, halve exposure for that week (a fast circuit-breaker for regime
  shocks the slow signal hasn't caught up to yet).

| Config | CAGR | Sharpe | MaxDD | Calmar | Worst week | Weeks < −2% | FTMO pass? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| raw Portfolio E (10% vol) | 4.5% | 0.76 | −12.2% | 0.37 | −5.1% | 1.7% | **no** |
| scaled to ~6% vol | 2.7% | 0.76 | −7.5% | 0.36 | −3.0% | 0.7% | YES |
| scaled ~6% + dd-scaling | 1.9% | 0.66 | −5.3% | 0.36 | −2.7% | 0.4% | YES |
| **scaled ~6% + dd + vol-brake** | **2.5%** | **0.94** | **−3.6%** | **0.68** | **−1.3%** | **0%** | **YES** |
| scaled to ~4% vol + dd | 1.5% | 0.70 | −4.0% | 0.37 | −1.9% | 0% | YES |

**All three mechanisms stacked wins on every axis, not just drawdown** — the
combined "6% vol + dd-scaling + vol-brake" config has the *highest* Sharpe
(0.94, up from 0.76 raw) and Calmar (0.68, nearly double raw) of any row, not
merely the smallest drawdown. That's because the vol brake specifically cuts
exposure during the weeks that would otherwise be the worst ones — removing
tail risk disproportionately improves the risk-adjusted number, not just the
worst case. Zero weeks below −2% under this config vs. 1.7% of weeks for the
raw portfolio. **This is the adopted production configuration.**

A daily-loss sanity check (the strategy trades weekly, so there's no explicit
daily model): at ~6% annual portfolio vol, weekly σ is ~0.8%; a 5% single-day
move would be a >5-sigma weekly-equivalent event, and per-sleeve caps (1x, no
leverage) bound how much a single overnight gap can move the book.

## Phase 12 — robustness: walk-forward, Monte Carlo, deflated Sharpe, perturbation

System under test: the Phase 11 adopted config (6% vol + dd-scaling +
vol-brake). No parameter is fit anywhere in this phase — every check below is
either out-of-sample by construction or a stress on execution assumptions.

**1. Disjoint 5-year windows** (no walk-forward *fitting*, since nothing was
ever fit — this measures whether the effect is stable through different
market regimes):

| Window | Sharpe | CAGR | MaxDD |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2006–2011 (GFC) | 0.79 | 2% | −3% |
| 2011–2016 | 0.78 | 2% | −3% |
| 2016–2021 (COVID) | 1.18 | 3% | −4% |
| 2021–2025 | 0.93 | 2% | −3% |

Every single window is positive, and the range (0.78–1.18) is tight — no
regime dependency where the whole edge came from one period.

**2. Monte Carlo — 26-week block bootstrap, 2000 resampled paths** (blocks
preserve within-block autocorrelation rather than reshuffling weeks
independently, which would destroy the trend-persistence structure being
tested):

- Sharpe: 5th percentile 0.58, median 0.91, 95th percentile 1.26.
- MaxDD: median −4.6%, 95th-percentile-worst −6.9%.
- **P(maxDD worse than −10%) = 0.2%** — the FTMO 10% line is a genuine tail
  event under this config, not a coin flip.

**3. Execution stress** — signal delayed an extra week, and per-turnover
costs added directly to sleeve returns:

| Stress | Sharpe | CAGR | MaxDD |
|---|---|---|---|
| clean | 0.94 | 2% | −4% |
| +1 week execution delay | 0.98 | 3% | −4% |
| 10bp per turn | 0.91 | 2% | −4% |
| 25bp per turn (spread stress) | 0.88 | 2% | −4% |
| delay + 25bp combined | 0.92 | 2% | −4% |

Barely moves. A slow weekly system with ~10 trades/asset/decade has very
little turnover to tax, and a 1-week delay doesn't matter because trend
episodes last many weeks. This is a structural strength of the *slowness* of
the system, not something separately engineered.

**4. Deflated Sharpe Ratio (Bailey & López de Prado).** The observed Sharpe
of 0.94 is corrected for the fact that ~500 distinct configurations were
examined across Phases 3–11 (fast/slow/MA-kind grid, signal families, sizing
schemes, portfolios, filters, entries, exits, overlays) — the more
configurations tried, the more likely *some* of them look good by chance
alone, and DSR asks "what's the probability the *true* Sharpe is still
positive after accounting for that search?" Formula in
`src/phase12_robustness.py::deflated_sharpe`, using the expected-maximum-Sharpe-under-noise
correction with the trial series' own skew and kurtosis.

**DSR = 0.875**, computed over 1,068 weeks. This is **below the conventional
0.9 "safe" threshold**, and is reported as-is rather than rounded up or
explained away — the edge is probably real (an 87.5% probability its true
Sharpe exceeds zero, after correcting for the whole research program's
multiple-testing exposure) but is not proven beyond reasonable doubt. This is
the single most important caveat in the whole program and is carried into
`engine/config.json`'s `caveats` block verbatim.

**5. Parameter perturbation (±20%):**

| Perturbation | Sharpe | CAGR | MaxDD |
|---|---|---|---|
| fast=22 slow=55 target=10% (base) | 0.94 | 2% | −4% |
| fast=18 slow=44 | 0.88 | 2% | −4% |
| fast=26 slow=66 | 0.92 | 2% | −4% |
| fast=18 slow=66 | 0.92 | 2% | −4% |
| fast=26 slow=44 | 0.87 | 2% | −4% |
| target=8% | 0.89 | 2% | −3% |
| target=12% | 0.93 | 3% | −4% |

Every perturbed variant stays within 0.07 Sharpe of the base config — no
cliff nearby, consistent with Phase 3's flat-plateau finding at the signal
level.

**Overall verdict:** PASS on stability, cost/delay insensitivity, and
parameter insensitivity. **Caveated** on statistical significance (DSR 0.875
< 0.9) — hence "deploy small, paper-trade first" rather than "deploy at
full size," which is exactly what `engine/config.json`'s caveats block says.

## Phase 13 — combining with mean reversion

**MR sleeve** (`src/phase13_combine_mr.py::mr_returns`), deliberately simple
— the goal here is measuring correlation structure, not building a competing
MR system (that's the separate Mean-Reversion-Research program):

- Universe: 8 equity index/sector sleeves (SPY, QQQ, DIA, IWM, NAS100, SP500,
  TECH, INDUSTRIALS).
- Entry: `close < SMA(26)` AND `2-week return < −5%` (a panic dip) AND
  `close > SMA(55) × 0.85` (still inside a non-crashed market — this isn't a
  falling-knife catcher).
- Exit: `close > SMA(26)` or after 4 weeks, whichever first.
- Vol-matched to the TF portfolio's standard deviation before blending, so
  the comparison is apples-to-apples on risk, not just raw return.

**Correlation TF vs MR: 0.25** — genuinely low, exactly the kind of
diversification the strategy-mixing premise predicts (trend following wins on
sustained directional moves, mean reversion wins on panic dips and chop —
different market conditions).

| Blend | Sharpe | CAGR | MaxDD | Calmar |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TF 100% / MR 0% | 0.94 | 2% | −4% | 0.68 |
| TF 80% / MR 20% | 0.91 | 2% | −4% | 0.49 |
| TF 70% / MR 30% | 0.87 | 2% | −5% | 0.41 |
| TF 50% / MR 50% | 0.73 | 2% | −5% | 0.28 |
| TF 0% / MR 100% | 0.22 | 1% | −8% | 0.07 |

**Every blend ratio is worse than pure TF.** The low correlation is real, but
this particular MR sleeve's standalone Sharpe (0.22) is too weak relative to
TF's (0.94) for diversification to pay for the return it dilutes — the
math of blending two return streams only helps when both legs are
individually decent; a weak leg just drags the average down even at low
correlation. **Conclusion: do not blend with this sleeve.** The right move is
waiting for a properly-researched MR sleeve (the dedicated Mean-Reversion-Research
program) with its own Sharpe in the 0.7+ range before revisiting portfolio
allocation between the two styles.

## The production engine — what actually runs

`engine/config.json` is the single source of truth; every block has a
`_source` field citing the phase above that selected it. Pipeline
(`engine/core.py`):

```
data (src/data.py, weekly OHLC, 16 sleeves)
  -> sleeve_weight()          Phase 3+4: 22/55 SMA signal
                               Phase 5: EWMA-26 vol target 10%, cap 1x
  -> equal-weight portfolio    Phase 6
  -> overlay() exposure        Phase 11: 0.6x scale, dd-linear-cut @ -8%, vol brake
  -> RebalanceIntent list       one target weight per sleeve
  -> connector.execute()        DryRunConnector: journals + prints (see below)
```

`engine/run_weekly.py` runs this end to end: refresh data, compute targets,
run `ftmo_check` (halts new entries at 80% of FTMO's daily-loss and
total-drawdown caps as a hard circuit breaker independent of the backtest
overlays), journal the decision, execute.

**Why there's no live MT5 connector yet.** The original architecture calls
for an MT5 connector, and the *interface* for one exists
(`DryRunConnector.execute(intents) -> list[dict]`, easy to implement a second
class against) — but no working live-order code is committed. Writing broker
order-routing logic against symbol names nobody has verified against an
actual FTMO account would look finished and misfire the first time it's
actually used. That code gets written once there's a demo or funded account
to test it against; see the README's engine section for what's needed to get
there.

## Every test — what each one actually checks

**`src/test_backtest.py`** — sanity-checks the core backtest mechanics on
synthetic, hand-constructed price paths (not historical data, so the expected
answer is known exactly):

- `test_uptrend_goes_long_and_wins` — feeds a pure linear uptrend; asserts the
  system ends up long and captures most of the trend (equity > 1.5x). Catches
  sign errors in the crossover logic.
- `test_downtrend_stays_flat` — pure downtrend; asserts the system is *never*
  long and every return is exactly zero. Catches the single most dangerous
  possible bug (a long-only system accidentally going short or holding
  through a downtrend).
- `test_round_trip_trade_prices` — an up-then-down series that produces
  exactly one closed trade; asserts the logged entry/exit prices match the
  actual open prices at those dates, and that the trade is net positive.
  Catches off-by-one errors in the execution-lag shift (the 2-week signal
  delay is the easiest place in the whole codebase to get wrong, since it's
  silent — wrong numbers, not a crash).

**`engine/test_engine.py`** — checks the production pipeline against both the
research-validated numbers and hand-computed risk-control edges:

- `test_engine_end_to_end` — runs the full config-driven pipeline and asserts
  Sharpe > 0.5 and maxDD > −10% (i.e., inside the FTMO line) against real
  market data; asserts weights are long-only and sum to ≤ 1 (no accidental
  leverage). This is a regression guard — if a future edit to `core.py`
  quietly breaks the sizing math, this is what catches it before it reaches
  `run_weekly.py`.
- `test_ftmo_check_halts` — three hand-computed scenarios: no loss (must not
  halt), a −4.5% single-day move (must halt, since it's past 80% of the 5%
  daily cap), and a −9% drawdown from a −8.5% peak-adjusted equity mark (must
  halt, since it's past 80% of the 10% total cap). This is the one test in
  the whole repo that exists purely to prevent silent risk-limit failures —
  if this test ever passes with wrong logic, the engine could trade past the
  point FTMO would terminate the account.
- `test_dry_run_journals_orders` — round-trips two synthetic `RebalanceIntent`
  objects through `DryRunConnector` and asserts both were journaled correctly
  with the right target weights. Confirms the journal (the engine's only
  persistent state) actually persists what the engine decided, since nothing
  downstream can be trusted if the journal silently drops or corrupts
  records.

Run everything: `python src/test_backtest.py && python engine/test_engine.py`.
